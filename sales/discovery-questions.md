# Discovery Questions — Sentinel Ops

## Purpose
Discovery is where we qualify the prospect, understand their actual pain, and build enough context to propose the right solution. Don't rush this — the quality of your discovery call determines whether you close the deal.

The goal is to understand:
1. What their actual security situation looks like
2. What pain they're feeling (or what event is driving interest)
3. Whether we can genuinely help them
4. Whether they have budget and decision-making clarity

---

## Stage 1: Company Context (5 min)

Start broad, get specific.

**"Can you tell me a bit about your business — what do you do?"**
- Establish what they do, their market, their size
- This helps you frame security in their language

**"How many people are on your team?"**
- Gauge company size (micro, SME, mid-market)
- Affects pricing tier and contract terms

**"What's your infrastructure look like? Do you have servers, cloud, or both?"**
- Get the technical lay of the land
- Don't dive deep yet — just the shape

**"Do you have an internal IT team or do you work with an external IT vendor?"**
- This tells you who your champion is and who to bring in
- If they have an IT vendor, we need to make sure that vendor is comfortable with us

---

## Stage 2: Security Situation (10 min)

This is where you learn what they're actually dealing with.

**"What prompted the conversation about security right now?"**
- Specific event (breach, near-miss, audit)
- Proactive (client requirement, board directive)
- Regulatory pressure (PDPA, BNM, MCMC)
- General concern without specific trigger

**"Have you had any security incidents or scares in the past? If so, what happened?"**
- This tells you what they're afraid of
- If they've already been burned, urgency is higher

**"What does your team currently do for security?"**
- Tools they use (antivirus, cloud native, monitoring)
- What they don't know they don't know
- Where the gaps are

**"How do you currently know if something goes wrong on your servers?"**
- If they say "we don't" — that's a strong signal
- If they have some monitoring, ask what

**"If you had a security incident right now — say a server got compromised — how would you know and what would you do?"**
- Get their current incident response story
- If they don't have one, that's a strong entry point

---

## Stage 3: Compliance and Risk (5 min)

**"Are you subject to any regulations that have a security or data component?"**
- PDPA (most Malaysian SMEs)
- BNM (financial sector)
- MCMC (telecommunications)
- ISO 27001 (if they have cert)
- Client security questionnaires (enterprise clients)

**"Have you ever been asked by a client or partner to prove your security posture?"**
- Security questionnaires, audit requirements
- This tends to create urgency

**"What would a security incident cost your business?"**
- Not to scare them — to calibrate urgency
- If they're small, they might not have thought about this

---

## Stage 4: Decision and Timeline (5 min)

**"Who else would be involved in a decision like this?"**
- Find out if they're the decision-maker or if there's someone above them
- If there's a boss or board involved, we need to give them the right materials

**"What's your timeline? Is there a deadline or event driving this?"**
- Client requiring security cert
- PDPA audit
- Merger/acquisition
- Board review
- No specific deadline — "when it makes sense"

**"What would success look like for you in 3 months?"**
- Get them to articulate what they want to achieve
- This helps you define what you deliver

**"If we could show you that we found something real on your servers — something you didn't know about — does that help you make a decision?"**
- Test their willingness to engage based on concrete findings
- Most people say yes — if they don't, probe why

---

## Red Flags (Disqualifiers)

These don't necessarily kill the deal, but they mean you need to adjust expectations:

- **No budget or unclear budget** — if they can't tell you what they can spend, it's hard to close
- **No decision-maker access** — if they need approval from someone you can't talk to, the process will stall
- **No real pain** — if they're just "window shopping," they won't close
- **Expecting us to replace their IT vendor** — we're a security overlay, not an IT replacement
- **Looking for a checkbox** — if they just want a report to show someone, we can do that but it's not ongoing MRR

---

## Notes

- **Don't pitch during discovery.** Your job is to ask questions, listen, and learn. You pitch after you understand.
- **Silence is fine.** Let them think. Don't rush to fill quiet space.
- **Write down what they say.** You'll use it later in your proposal and demo.
- **When in doubt, ask "why is that?"** It surfaces the real driver.
