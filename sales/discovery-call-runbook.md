# Sentinel Ops — Discovery Call Runbook

## Purpose
The discovery call is not a technical deep-dive. Its job is to determine:
- whether the prospect has a real pain worth solving
- whether Sentinel Ops is a fit
- whether a limited 30-day pilot is commercially realistic

## Discovery Call Goal
Get enough signal to decide one of three outcomes:
1. advance to pilot proposal
2. schedule technical follow-up
3. disqualify politely

## Target Call Length
20 to 30 minutes.

## Call Structure
### 1. Opening
Use:
"I’m not here to sell you a giant security transformation. I want to understand your environment, where the visibility gaps are, and whether a limited pilot would be useful."

### 2. Environment Questions
Ask:
- What kind of infrastructure are you running today?
- How many Linux servers or cloud workloads are in scope?
- Is your environment mostly cloud, on-prem, or hybrid?
- Who owns security operations today?
- Do you already collect logs or use any monitoring tools?

### 3. Pain Questions
Ask:
- What is frustrating you most right now?
- Are alerts being ignored because they are too noisy?
- Do you feel blind on server security or user activity?
- Have customers, auditors, or partners started asking security questions?
- Have you had any recent scare, incident, or near-miss?

### 4. Readiness Questions
Ask:
- Do you have a small set of critical systems we could start with?
- Is there a technical contact who can help with onboarding?
- Would a 30-day limited-scope pilot be easier to approve than a full managed service?
- Who would sign off on a pilot?
- What timeline are you working with?

### 5. Commercial Questions
Ask:
- Are you comparing this with hiring internally, using an MSSP, or doing nothing for now?
- Is there already budget for security monitoring, compliance, or infrastructure hardening?
- If the pilot shows value, what would make ongoing monthly service easy to justify internally?

## What Good Prospects Sound Like
Good prospects usually say things like:
- "We know we need this but nobody owns it properly."
- "We have logs, but nobody really watches them."
- "We’re under customer or audit pressure."
- "We need something practical without hiring a SOC."
- "We can start with a few critical servers first."

## Red Flags
Be careful if you hear:
- "Can you just cover everything for one low price?"
- "We need 24/7 SOC and incident response included."
- "We have no idea what systems we want included."
- "We want a full compliance solution immediately."
- "Can you do all of this before we decide whether to pay?"

These are signs of bad fit, scope risk, or low seriousness.

## Qualification Checklist
A prospect is qualified for a pilot if most of these are true:
- has 3 to 10 suitable systems for a pilot
- feels real pain around security visibility or posture
- has someone who can support onboarding
- has a reason to act in the next 30 to 90 days
- understands this is a limited managed pilot, not a miracle platform
- can pay a modest pilot fee without drama

## Close Options
### If qualified
Say:
"This sounds like a good fit for a limited pilot. I’ll put together a scoped proposal focused on a small number of critical systems so we can show value quickly."

### If partially qualified
Say:
"There may be a fit here, but I want to tighten the scope before proposing anything. Let’s confirm the systems and priorities first."

### If not qualified
Say:
"I don’t think it would be responsible to force a pilot here before the scope and ownership are clearer. Better to tighten that first than sell you something messy."

## Internal Notes To Capture After Call
Document immediately:
- company name
- industry
- infra type
- estimated host count
- main pain points
- urgency level
- buyer/contact names
- pilot fit: yes / maybe / no
- next step

## Discovery Principle
If the prospect wants a giant solution, narrow them.
If the prospect is vague, force specifics.
If the prospect wants everything cheap, qualify harder or walk.
